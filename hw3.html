Statistic Notions

Mean (Media)

The mean is a measure of central tendency, representing the average value in a dataset.

Formula:
Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)

Process:
Sum up all the data points and divide the total by the number of data points.
For example, if we have the numbers 2, 4, and 6, the mean is (2 + 4 + 6) / 3 = 4.

Variance (Varianza)

Variance measures how much the data points in a dataset differ from the mean. It shows the spread of the data.

Formula:
Variance = (Sum of squared differences from the mean) / (Number of values)

Process:
First, find the mean. Then, subtract the mean from each data point, square the result, and sum them up. Divide by the number of data points.
A higher variance indicates greater spread among the values.

Standard Deviation (Deviazione Standard)

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance. It provides a measure of the dispersion of the data relative to the mean.

Formula:
Standard Deviation = √Variance

Process:
Calculate the variance first, then take the square root of the result.
Standard deviation gives a more interpretable measure of spread compared to variance, as it is in the same units as the data.

Empirical Distribution (Distribuzione Empirica)

An empirical distribution represents the observed frequencies of data points, giving an approximation of the probability distribution based on the sample.

Process:
Collect the observed data points, group them into bins, and calculate the frequency of each bin.
The empirical distribution is often represented as a histogram.

Usage:
Empirical distributions help understand the actual spread and pattern of the data in a sample.

Central Limit Theorem (Teorema del Limite Centrale)

The Central Limit Theorem states that the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution as the sample size becomes large, regardless of the original data distribution.

Process:
Take random samples from a population and calculate their means.
As the number of samples increases, the distribution of these means will approach a normal distribution.

Usage:
This theorem is fundamental in inferential statistics, allowing us to make predictions about population parameters from sample statistics.

Binomial Distribution (Distribuzione Binomiale)

The binomial distribution models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials, each with the same probability of success.

Formula:
P(X=k) = (n choose k) * p^k * (1-p)^(n-k)
where n is the number of trials, k is the number of successes, and p is the probability of success.

Process:
Use the formula to calculate the probability of obtaining exactly k successes in n trials.
Binomial distributions are used when there are only two outcomes (e.g., success or failure).

Arithmetic Mean

The arithmetic mean is the most common measure of central tendency, representing the average of all values in a dataset.

Formula:
Arithmetic Mean = (Σxᵢ) / N
Where Σxᵢ is the sum of all values in the dataset, and N is the number of values.

Process:
1. Add up all the values in the dataset.
2. Divide the sum by the number of values (N).
The result is the arithmetic mean, a simple and effective way to describe the center of the data.

Mode

The mode is the value that appears most frequently in a dataset. It is a measure of central tendency that highlights the most common data point.

Process:
1. Count how often each value appears in the dataset.
2. The value with the highest frequency is the mode.
If multiple values have the same frequency, the dataset is multimodal, and all such values are considered modes.

Geometric Mean

The geometric mean is a measure of central tendency that is calculated as the nth root of the product of all values. It is useful for datasets involving growth rates or ratios.

Formula:
Geometric Mean (GM) = (Πxᵢ)^(1/N)
Where Πxᵢ is the product of all values, and N is the number of values.

Process:
1. Multiply all the values in the dataset together.
2. Take the Nth root of the product, where N is the number of values.
The geometric mean is particularly effective for datasets where values vary multiplicatively, such as financial returns.

Harmonic Mean

The harmonic mean is a measure of central tendency, particularly useful for rates. It is the reciprocal of the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals of the values.

Formula:
Harmonic Mean (HM) = N / Σ(1/xᵢ)
Where Σ(1/xᵢ) is the sum of the reciprocals of all values, and N is the total number of values.

Process:
1. Find the reciprocal of each value in the dataset.
2. Calculate the sum of these reciprocals.
3. Divide the number of values (N) by this sum.
The harmonic mean is particularly useful for situations involving rates, such as speed or efficiency.

Midrange

The midrange is a simple measure of central tendency, calculated as the average of the minimum and maximum values in a dataset.

Formula:
Midrange = (min + max) / 2
Where min is the smallest value and max is the largest value in the dataset.

Process:
1. Identify the minimum and maximum values in the dataset.
2. Add these two values together.
3. Divide the sum by 2 to find the midrange.
The midrange gives a quick estimate of the center but can be influenced by outliers.

Function: r ≤ p/n (Success Probability Check)

This function checks if an attack by a hacker is successful based on the probability of success (p) and the number of servers (n).

Formula:
r ≤ p/n
Where r is a random number between 0 and 1, p is the success probability, and n is the total number of servers.

Process:
1. A random number r is generated between 0 and 1.
2. The value of r is compared with p/n.
3. If r is less than or equal to p/n, the attack is successful; otherwise, it fails.
This approach makes it harder for hackers to succeed as the number of servers (n) increases, effectively reducing the success rate.

Variance Calculation Function

This function calculates the variance of successful attacks across hackers, which measures how spread out the successes are compared to the mean.

Formula:
Variance = (Σ(xᵢ - μ)²) / N
Where xᵢ represents the successes for each hacker, μ is the average number of successes, and N is the total number of hackers.

Process:
1. Calculate the mean (μ) of total successes across all hackers.
2. Subtract the mean from each hacker’s success count, then square the difference.
3. Sum all the squared differences and divide by the total number of hackers (N).
4. The result is the variance, representing how spread out the hacker success counts are relative to the mean.
